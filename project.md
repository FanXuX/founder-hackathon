# Project Plan

## Project Summary

Project name:

Founder Signal Triage

Product vision:

We are building Founder OS: a decision clarity layer for early-stage founders under signal overload.

Hackathon wedge:

Founder Signal Triage is the first wedge. It helps founders decide what deserves to become work right now.

One-line product definition:

> Task tools manage work after a decision is made. Founder Signal Triage helps founders decide what deserves to become work by remembering how similar signals affected their decisions before.

Pitch line:

> Every founder has pulled the same fire alarm before. Founder Signal Triage is the system that remembers, so this time you decide instead of react.

Most compressed positioning:

> Founder OS helps early-stage founders decide what deserves to become work, using their own decision history to protect judgment under signal overload.

Target user:

Early-stage founders, solo founders, and indie builders who face messy daily signals from product, market, investors, team, execution, and their own founder state.

Main problem:

Founders do not lack tools. They already have Notion, Linear, calendars, CRMs, Slack, and ChatGPT. The unresolved problem happens before task management:

> What actually deserves to become work right now?

Proposed solution:

Founder Signal Triage processes messy founder input, classifies signals, detects state-amplified reaction risk, recalls repeated decision patterns from founder history, and resets today's priority before the founder overreacts, drifts, or loses execution momentum.

Refined positioning:

> We are not solving task management. We are helping founders keep judgment under signal noise.

Final positioning:

> Founder OS is not another workspace, task manager, or AI coach. It starts as a decision clarity layer for solo and small-team early-stage founders operating under signal overload.

Founder decision intelligence / pre-task decision triage.

Founder OS can become the decision and focus layer above existing tools. Notion, Linear, CRMs, calendars, BI tools, and AI assistants remain systems of record or execution tools. Founder OS becomes the layer that decides what deserves founder attention, what can be delegated, and what should not become work.

## Founder Pain Points

- Founders receive too many weak signals at once: customer feedback, investor silence, competitor moves, teammate requests, deadlines, and emotional pressure.
- Existing tools manage tasks after decisions are made, but they do not help decide what should become work.
- Founders often overreact to loud external signals, especially when tired, anxious, or under deadline pressure.
- Founder state is not therapy in this product. It is treated as a decision-quality risk.
- Similar decision patterns repeat, but current tools do not remember how previous reactions turned out.
- Without pattern memory, founders may keep pulling the same fire alarm and turning the wrong signal into work.
- AI and SaaS tools now create more input, summaries, and suggestions, but they do not create equal judgment clarity.
- Solo and bootstrapped founders lose weeks to priority paralysis: debating features, growth hacks, fundraising, and product direction without a system that is opinionated about what matters now.
- Founder time is runway. Every hour spent on the wrong task is runway burned in disguise.
- Founders often default to the work they like or can control, such as building more features, instead of the work the company most needs.
- Many founders become the only memory layer of the company: past decisions, context, failed experiments, and relationship history live in their head.

Core pain statement:

> Early founders do not lack information or tools. They lack a system that protects their judgment when signals, pressure, and founder state collide.

Sharper pain statement:

> Early-stage founders do not lack tools. They lack decision clarity under signal overload.

What this means:

- The problem is not productivity in general.
- The problem is not task storage.
- The problem is not wellness.
- The problem is decision bandwidth, signal filtering, priority reset, and founder-specific decision memory.

## Competitive Landscape

### Task And Project Tools

Examples: Notion, Linear, Jira, Asana, ClickUp, Monday.

What they solve:

- Store tasks, documents, projects, roadmaps, sprints, and workflows.
- Help teams coordinate after work has been defined.

What they do not solve:

- They do not decide what deserves to become a task.
- They do not understand founder-level judgment or decision fatigue.
- They do not learn from past founder decisions and outcomes.
- They do not distinguish founder-owned decisions from delegable work.

### AI Assistants

Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Notion AI.

What they solve:

- Generate analysis, plans, drafts, summaries, market research, and decision options.

What they do not solve:

- They are usually stateless unless the founder manually builds memory.
- They do not bind advice to execution, ownership, or decision history.
- They do not remember whether similar past reactions were useful or costly.

### Calendar And Email AI

Examples: Motion, Clockwise, Reclaim, Superhuman AI, Gmail Copilot.

What they solve:

- Schedule tasks, protect focus time, summarize email, and generate replies.

What they do not solve:

- They optimize time blocks, not strategic judgment.
- They do not know which signals should interrupt the founder.
- They can fill a day without answering whether the founder is working on the right thing.

### Founder Tools

Examples: pitch tools, investor CRMs, fundraising tools, validation tools, product feedback tools.

What they solve:

- Improve one workflow: deck creation, investor tracking, customer feedback, idea validation, or research.

What they do not solve:

- They remain point tools.
- They do not connect investor, product, team, market, execution, and founder-state signals into a single decision layer.
- They do not answer: what should the founder personally focus on today?

### Wellness, Coaching, And Journaling

Examples: coaching apps, therapy apps, meditation tools, AI journaling tools, founder mental health programs.

What they solve:

- Support reflection, stress, resilience, journaling, and emotional awareness.

What they do not solve:

- They rarely connect founder state to decision quality.
- They do not connect emotional state to roadmap, fundraising, hiring, or execution decisions.
- They are usually private reflection spaces, not operating layers.

### AI Productivity And OS-Like Tools

Examples: Taskade Founder OS, Coda, ClickUp AI, Notion AI, Foundra, Zapier AI.

What they solve:

- Combine documents, tasks, automation, collaboration, and AI workflows.

What they do not solve:

- They are usually workspace-first, not founder-judgment-first.
- They do not clearly productize the boundary between founder ownership, team delegation, and AI execution.
- They still tend to increase surface area instead of reducing decision load.

## Founder Operating Framework

Founder OS should not tell founders who they are. It should help founders build, calibrate, and run their own judgment system.

Default framework:

- Signal reaction patterns: competitor-triggered pivot impulse, investor silence anxiety, negative user feedback spiral, advisor opinion over-weighting, positive feedback overconfidence, market report scope expansion.
- Decision style patterns: over-analysis before reversible decisions, premature commitment under excitement, avoidance of uncomfortable stakeholder decisions, perfectionism before public release, low tolerance for ambiguity, over-indexing on external validation.
- Execution patterns: scope creep near deadline, starting new work before closing the current loop, polishing instead of shipping, delaying delegation, doing low-leverage work personally, reopening decisions after they were already made.
- Founder state patterns: sleep-deprived strategic doubt, anxious fundraising reaction, excitement-driven expansion, fatigue-driven meaning collapse, rejection-triggered repositioning, pressure-driven control tightening.
- Ownership patterns: founder bottleneck, delegation resistance, agent distrust, excessive review ownership, unclear decision rights, team decision dependency.

Founder Operating Profile v1:

- Current stage.
- North Star.
- High-risk decision zones.
- Common triggers.
- Decision style.
- Default intervention style.
- Decisions founder must own.
- Decisions system can route away.
- Initial pattern hypotheses.

Pattern maturity:

| Stage | Condition | System voice |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Default | No or little history | "This may be..." |
| Hypothesis | 2 similar triggers and 1 founder confirmation | "A possible pattern is emerging..." |
| Validated | 3 similar triggers, 2 confirmations, and 1 recorded outcome | "This is a validated pattern..." |
| Operating Rule | Founder explicitly approves rule conversion | "Apply rule..." |

Pattern maturity is not simple data volume. It is recurrence plus founder confirmation plus outcome evidence plus decision impact.

Product principle:

> Patterns are hypotheses until the founder confirms them and outcomes validate them.

Demo profile:

```txt
Founder Operating Profile v1
Stage: MVP validation
North Star: Validate onboarding clarity and first paid use case
High-risk pattern: competitor-triggered pivot impulse
Decision style: fast to react to external validation signals
Intervention style: challenge premature pivots and protect focus
Standing rule: no roadmap decision while exhausted or before user evidence is checked
Pattern maturity: Validated
```

Demo recommendation:

> Based on your current North Star and validated competitor-triggered pivot pattern, this is not a pivot moment. It is an onboarding validation and scope-control moment.

Guardrails:

- Do not use personality labels.
- Do not say "you are an avoidant founder" or "you are a reactive founder."
- Say "this resembles a possible operating pattern" or "this is a validated decision pattern."
- Every pattern must be editable, confirmable, rejectable, mergeable, splittable, and eventually convertible into a rule.
- Patterns should connect to outcomes, not only self-description.
- The goal is not insight for its own sake. The goal is converting repeated decision history into operating rules.

Key product principle:

> Decision history becomes input for future prioritization.

Competitive claim:

> Existing Founder OS products are mostly workspaces, templates, CRM layers, or decision archives. They help founders organize work, but they do not continuously improve the founder's decision loop.

Strongest competitive line:

> No tool truly owns the decision -> execution -> feedback -> learning loop for the founder seat.

Taskade response:

> Taskade manages work. Founder OS protects the founder's decision layer.

Decision Ledger response:

> Decision Ledger stores decisions. Founder OS uses decision memory to drive today's operating behavior.

## Decision Intelligence Gap

Existing decision intelligence tools mostly serve enterprises and departments. They aggregate data, produce dashboards, run simulations, and recommend business optimizations.

The gap for founders:

- A founder has limited bandwidth and incomplete data.
- The hard part is not getting more information.
- The hard part is filtering noise, reducing decision count, and protecting judgment under pressure.

Founder Signal Triage focuses on decision-to-focus:

```txt
messy signals -> decision risk -> founder memory -> one priority -> next action
```

This is different from enterprise decision intelligence:

```txt
data aggregation -> analysis -> dashboard -> recommendation
```

The product should do subtraction, not just analysis.

It should help the founder answer:

- What should interrupt me?
- What can wait?
- What am I overreacting to?
- What have I done in similar moments before?
- What deserves to become work today?

## Founder State As Decision Risk

Founder state is not treated as therapy or wellness.

It is a decision-quality input.

The product should recognize that exhaustion, anxiety, excitement, over-control, and self-doubt can distort founder judgment.

Examples:

- Exhaustion plus competitor news can create pivot impulse.
- Anxiety plus investor silence can create premature narrative changes.
- Excitement plus one positive customer call can create overbuilding.
- Stress plus team uncertainty can create over-control or delayed delegation.

Product rule:

> Founder state changes what should be allowed into the decision queue today.

The product should not say:

> You are anxious.

It should say:

> Decision risk is high. Do not make roadmap decisions from this state. Check user evidence first.

## State-Amplified Reaction Logic

The system should distinguish between:

1. Real external signal.
2. Founder reaction to that signal.
3. Risk that the reaction is amplified by current founder state.

Example:

```txt
Signal: Competitor launched a new feature
Signal type: Real external market signal
Reaction: "Maybe we should pivot"
Risk: State-amplified overreaction
Recommendation: Do not make roadmap decisions while exhausted. Check user evidence first.
```

Do not call signals fake.

Use precise language:

- `real_signal`
- `state_amplified_reaction`
- `decision_distortion_risk`

## Pattern Memory

Pattern memory is the core differentiator.

The system should use pre-seeded founder history to detect repeated decision patterns.

Pattern memory is not AI remembering chat history. It is the system remembering structured decision history:

```txt
trigger -> state -> decision -> action -> outcome -> pattern
```

Example:

```txt
Pattern detected: Competitor-triggered pivot impulse

This is the 3rd time competitor activity triggered a pivot impulse before user evidence was checked.

Jan 12: Competitor launched -> considered pivot -> stayed course -> correct
Mar 18: Market report -> expanded roadmap -> cut back after 5 days -> costly
May 08: Advisor suggested enterprise pivot -> checked evidence -> SMB stayed correct
```

The pattern output should include:

1. Pattern name.
2. One insight sentence.
3. Timeline trace.
4. What to do differently today.

Example insight:

> When competitors or advisors challenge your direction, you tend to question positioning before checking user evidence.

Early-stage founders and solo founders first. Later expansion could include founder teams, accelerators, founder coaches, and startup programs.
